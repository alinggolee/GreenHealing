Course Introduction: What Is Green Healing?

## Learning Goals

> By the end of this week, students will be able to:

- describe the basic idea of green healing in simple English.
- explain why this course connects nature, wellbeing, and helping relationships.
- understand the course structure, including experience, reflection, program design, and field practice.
- identify how this course is connected to SDG15, self-care, and social engagement.
- begin reflecting on their own relationship with the environment.

## Content

This week introduces the course Green Healing and Program Design.
Green healing is not only about plants, outdoor activities, or feeling relaxed in nature. It is also about the relationship between self, others, and the environment. In this course, nature is not just a background. It becomes a way to think, feel, connect, and care.
This course brings together green healing experience, reflective learning, program design, and field practice. Students will not only learn ideas in class. They will also move through a learning process that connects experience with action:
experience → reflection → design → action → reflection again
This course also connects with SDG15, which reminds us that caring for the land is also connected to caring for people, communities, and wellbeing.
In this first week, the main goal is to build a starting point. Before designing activities for others, students first begin by noticing their own relationship with nature, daily life, and wellbeing.

## O — Orientation

When do you feel supported by nature?
Maybe it is when you sit quietly under a tree. Maybe it is when you smell grass after rain, drink warm tea, hear insects at night, or walk slowly in a place that helps you breathe more deeply.
Green healing often starts from very small experiences. It does not always begin with a big activity. Sometimes it begins with noticing.
So this week is a starting point. We begin by asking a simple question:
What does nature mean in my life right now?

## D — Delivery

Green healing is not only about “going outside” or “doing something with plants.” It is about understanding how natural environments can support people’s physical, emotional, and relational wellbeing.
This course is also not only about learning ideas. It is about learning how to connect ideas with practice. That is why the course includes four important parts:
experience
reflection
program design
field practice
You will learn by experiencing, thinking, discussing, designing, and trying things in real settings. In other words, this course values both thinking and doing.
Another important idea is that green healing is connected to relationships. It asks us to think about questions like these:
How do I relate to the environment?
How do natural spaces affect emotions and wellbeing?
How can a healing activity support not only myself, but also other people?
This is also why the course is connected to SDG15. Caring for land, ecology, and living environments is not separate from human care. Environmental care and human wellbeing are deeply connected.

## I — Interaction

Now take a moment to think about your own life.
What places make you feel calm?
What kind of natural environment helps you feel safe, quiet, or supported?
When you feel tired or stressed, what kind of place helps you recover?
Can nature become part of helping work? Why?
Start with your own experience. Then listen to other people’s experiences. You may notice that healing does not look exactly the same for everyone. For one person, it may be sunlight. For another, it may be water, trees, herbs, wind, or simply a slower pace.
As you listen, try to notice both similarities and differences. This is important, because later in the course, green healing will not only be about “what I like,” but also about “how to design meaningful experiences for others.”

## R — Recap

Green healing is not just an activity. It is a way of understanding the relationship between people and environment, and also between care and action.
In this course, learning will not stop at knowledge. You will move between experience, reflection, design, and practice. This means that your own observations and feelings are part of the learning process.
So in Week 1, the most important thing is not to give a perfect definition. The important thing is to begin noticing:
What kind of environment supports me?
What does healing mean in daily life?
How might nature become part of care, learning, and connection?
These questions will continue to grow throughout the course.
