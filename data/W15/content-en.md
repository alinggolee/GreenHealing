# Week 15 ｜PBL Phase I: Integrating Design Thinking into Green Healing Project Design


## Learning Goals

- By the end of this session, students will be able to:
- understand the core idea of PBL (Problem-Based Learning) in project development.
- apply empathy and observation to identify adolescent girls’ needs.
- define a clear problem statement for a Green Healing project.
- integrate empathy, adolescent development, and green healing knowledge into a project concept.

- Introduction
- This session marks the beginning of the final project preparation.
- Students move from experience-based learning to problem-based project design, focusing on real needs of adolescent girls.
- The goal is to develop a Green Healing Project that is meaningful, practical, and socially responsive.

“What challenges do adolescent girls face in daily life?”
Teacher introduces:
PBL = learning through solving real-world problems
Design Thinking = understanding people before solving problems
Teacher guiding language:
“We have learned how to understand others and ourselves. Now, we begin to design for real people.”

## D — Delivery

Introduction to Design Thinking (Phase 1–2):
Empathize (understand users)
Define (identify the problem)
Focus topics:
menstrual care
stress and emotional pressure
body image
school-life balance
Teacher explains how:
empathy map + persona → project design
green healing → a structured helping approach

## I — Interaction

Students work in groups to:
Create a simple User Profile (Persona)
Who is she?
What problems does she face?
What does she need?
Identify key needs:
emotional
physical
social
Write a Problem Statement:
👉 “Adolescent girls need ______ because ______.”
Begin:
👉 Green Healing Project Design (Draft Concept)

## R — Recap

Each group shares one problem statement.
Teacher feedback focuses on:
clarity
relevance
feasibility
Teacher closing guidance:
“Your design should come from real understanding, not imagination.”

## ESP Vocabulary & Professional Usage

Empathy: understanding others’ feelings
Persona: a model user
Problem Statement: a clear description of an issue
User Needs: what people require
Intervention: a way to provide support
Example:
“Our persona experiences stress due to academic pressure.”
